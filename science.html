The big picture

Stress made us who we are. As individuals, but also as a specie, we’ve evolved as a result of stress. Our body is designed to handle stress - ‘to flight from’ or to fight’ a life danger, such as an encounter with a wild animal. But in the modern world, the ‘faster is better’, the “always on” trends put us in a permanent alert state. This continuous stress became the norm and it turned against us.

Types of Stress

Acute Stress


There is a dog who wants to bite you or you are about to give a public speech. Within seconds, a hormonal cascade enters into action, your body machinery switches on a “crisis” state. To save your life, your body needs to produce more energy: more glucose is released from liver storage, more blood and oxygen are pumped to the heart, muscle and brain, you need to perform better than ever (running, speaking). Organs and functions which are not immediately needed (like digestion, sex, immunity) are slowed down. You feel your heart beating in your throat, you become hyper aware of everything around you, and feel boosted.


This ‘on the spot’ type of stress can be good for you because the stress hormones released help your mind and body to deal with the situation.


Stress can be positive and stimulating, keeping us alert and ready to avoid danger.

After consuming energy to save your live from a dangerous situation, your body needs to recover.

Chronic stress


There are no nasty dogs who wants to bite you every day. Luckily for us, because our stress response system (our ‘alert mode’) is not designed to be constantly “on”. We need to refuel our batteries


But if you are exposed too often or too much to stressful situations, without the necessary recovery or relaxation, your body will build-up stress-related tensions. This type of stress can cause wear and tear on your mind and body. The repeated/chronic stress will become negative and will then manifest in a variety of ways.

Biology (just a little bit)

In response to stress, the level of various hormones and molecules changes, especially those secreted by the adrenals, the ‘stress glands’.

Adrenals are 2 tinny pyramids on top of your kidneys. They secrete cortisol and DHEA, steroids hormones and other molecules (“catecholamines” lle adrenaline, noradrenaline) involved in stress response. They influence glucose, insulin and inflammation, and play a major role in bone and muscle building, mood and mental focus, stamina, sex drive and sleep cycles.

When stress is prolonged, adrenals start playing the rolling coaster, become overworked, they get tired, lack of juice and will conduct to exhaustion and burnout, along with numerous aches: blood sugar and insulin imbalances, food cravings, weight gain (belly fat), sleep disturbances, low progesterone, high cholesterol, low thyroid, low metabolism, low immunity.

Cortisol (stress hormone)

Also called the „stress hormone“ generally affects the body‘s adaptation to stimuli. An important function of cortisol is that it activates the metabolism to mobilise energy reserves. Cortisol influences emotional stress, blood pressure, metabolism, immune response and memory formation, among other important functions.

DHEA (youth hormone)

DHEA is made of cholesterol, mainly in the adrenal gland, the same gland which is secreting the cortisol. DHEA is counteracting the cortisol, it balances the stress reaction caused by cortisol thus helps dealing with stress.

The production of DHEA decreases continually with age, from age 25 on. Not only that, DHEA is also the precursor of the sex hormones testosterone and oestrogen.

Adrenaline (performance and stress hormone)

When a stressful situation occurs, adrenaline enables the body to react to increased demands: it increases the respiratory volume, blood pressure and the heart rate, providing the body with more oxygen; it increases attentiveness and the general mental activity, motivation and willingness to perform. In addition, it increases the metabolic activity and ensures that very quickly the muscles and the brain have more energy available.

On the other hand, it inhibits digestion and sexual activity.

Serotonin (mood messenger / happiness hormone)

Serotonin, also called the „happiness hormone“, is an important messenger substance in the brain and is mainly created in the central nervous system. Serotonin is essentially responsible for our emotions. Acting together with adrenaline and dopamine, it elevates the mood and controls motivation. It also has a relaxing and sleep-enhancing and anti-depressive effect. In addition, it participates in regulating the feeling of satiety and sensitivity to pain. Essential functions of the intestine and the absorption of nutrients through the intestines are also affected by serotonin.

Dopamine acts mostly in a stimulating manner. It is essential in particular for coordination, movement, memory and learning as well as concentration and mental performance. In addition, together with serotonin, it has a mood-lifting effect and regulates the so-called reward system and therefore drive and motivation.

Glutamate (natural stimulant and brain detoxifier)

Glutamate has various important functions in the brain. As a messenger substance it is important for learning and memory, for intentional, controlled movements, as well as for the ability of the brain to perceive the environment and to adapt to it. In addition, glutamate participates in the metabolic processes that detoxify the brain of damaging ammonia.

GABA (the body‘s natural sedative)

GABA has a calming effect, since it influences the amount of the stress hormones. It prevents sensory overload, has an effect that is anxiety-resolving, relaxing, sleep-enhancing, pain-reducing, anti-spasmodic and it stabilises blood pressure. In addition, GABA is very important for memory and learning.

An elevated level of GABA occurs mostly as a counteracting agent when other messenger substances are activated in excess, as is the case during chronic stress. This is how the body attempts to balance the effects of the elevated stress hormones or to at least dampen them.

Other hormones modified by stress

Thyroid Hormones

Are slowed down during stressful conditions. T3 and T4 levels decrease with stress. Stress inhibits the thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) secretion through the action of glucocorticoids on the central nervous system.

It is an additional cause of fatigue, gaining weight, low mood with chronic stress.

Sex Hormones

Prolonged stress is leading to decreasing of reproduction function with low sex hormones.

It is an additional cause of fatigue, gaining weight, low mood with chronic stress.

Insulin

May decrease during stress, thus increasing the level of sugar in the blood.

Growth hormone

Is increases during acute physical stress. It may enhance the metabolic activity, but in chronic psychosocial stress is less secreted.

Why Measure Stress

Everyone knows what stress is, this buzzword is on everyone’s lips. But it is a big challenge to know where on the stress roller-coaster you are.

To understand what’s the real impact on your body of these stress molecules, you need to rely on more than what you “feel”. How you “perceive” the stress does not always reflect your biology.

Those stress molecules can be measured and they give you a clue on how to deal with stress from within.
