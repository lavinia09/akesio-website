The big picture

Stress made us who we are. As individuals, but also as a specie, we evolved as a result of stress. Our body is designed to handle stress - ‘to flight from’ or to fight’ a life danger - and it's useful when we encounter a wild animal. But in the modern world, the ‘faster is better’, the “always on” trends put us in a permanent alert state. This continuous stress became the norm and it turned against us.

Types of Stress

Acute Stress


A dog wants to bite you or you are about to give a public speech. Within seconds, a hormonal cascade enters into action, your body machinery switches on a “crisis” state. To save your life, your body needs to produce more energy: more glucose is released from liver storage, more blood and oxygen are pumped to the heart, muscle and brain, you need to perform better than ever (running, speaking). Organs and functions which are not immediately needed (like digestion, sex, immunity) are slowed down. You feel your heart beating in your throat, you become hyper aware of everything around you, and feel boosted.


This ‘on the spot’ type of stress can be good for you because the stress hormones released help your mind and body to deal with the situation.


Stress can be positive and stimulating, keeping us alert and ready to avoid danger.

Prolonged stress is harmful and can cause wear and tear on your mind and body.

Chronic stress


There are no nasty dogs who wants to bite you every day. Luckily for us, because our stress response system (our ‘alert mode’) is not designed to be constantly “on”. After consuming energy to save your live from a dangerous situation, your body needs to recover.


But if you are exposed too often or too much to stressful situations, without the necessary recovery or relaxation, your body will build-up stress-related tensions. This type of stress can cause wear and tear on your mind and body. The repeated/chronic stress will become negative and will then manifest in a variety of ways.

Biology (just a little bit)

In response to stress, the level of various hormones and molecules changes, especially those secreted by the adrenals, the ‘stress glands’.

Adrenals are 2 tinny pyramids on top of your kidneys. They secrete cortisol and DHEA, steroids hormones and other molecules (adrenaline, noradrenaline) involved in stress response. They influence glucose, insulin and inflammation, and play a major role in bone and muscle building, mood and mental focus, stamina, sex drive and sleep cycles.

When stress is prolonged, adrenals start playing the rolling coaster, become overworked, they get tired, lack of juice and will conduct to exhaustion and burnout, along with numerous aches: blood sugar and insulin imbalances, food cravings, weight gain (belly fat), sleep disturbances, low progesterone, high cholesterol, low thyroid, low metabolism, low immunity.

Adrenaline
(performance and stress hormone)

When a stressful situation occurs, adrenaline enables the body to react to increased demands: it increases the respiratory volume, blood pressure and the heart rate, providing the body with more oxygen; it increases attentiveness and the general mental activity, motivation and willingness to perform. In addition, it increases the metabolic activity and ensures that very quickly the muscles and the brain have more energy available.

On the other hand, it inhibits digestion and sexual activity.

Why Measure Stress

Everyone knows what stress is, but it's a big challenge to know where on the stress roller-coaster you are.

To understand what’s the real impact on your body of these stress molecules, you need to rely on more than what you “feel”. How you “perceive” the stress does not always reflect your biology.

Stress molecules can be objectively measured and this gives you a clue on how to deal with stress from within.
